In a town like **Kennett Square**, sports are more than just an after-school activity—they are a community heartbeat. From the Friday night lights at **Kennett High School** to the competitive clashes in the **Ches-Mont League**, our student-athletes are under immense pressure to perform.

But for athletes with **ADHD**, the challenge isn't just about physical talent or endurance. It's about managing a brain that is wired for high-intensity action but often struggles with the "invisible" side of the game: focus, emotional regulation, and the transition from the field back to the classroom.

### **The ADHD Advantage (and the ADHD Wall)**

Many of the world's most elite athletes have ADHD. Why? Because the ADHD brain thrives on novelty, urgency, and physical stimulation. Traits like **hyperfocus** and quick reactive decision-making are massive assets when you're a goalie in the crease or a midfielder looking for an open lane.

However, many Kennett-area athletes hit a "wall" when the structure of the game ends. They might struggle with:

- **Post-game spiraling:** Obsessing over a single mistake for hours (or days).

- **Executive function gaps:** Forgetting gear, missing practice times, or struggling to balance a heavy KHS academic load with a varsity schedule.

- **Emotional flooding:** Getting "too high" after a win or "too low" after a loss, making it hard to maintain consistency.

### **A Different Kind of Coaching: The "Bill Reichle" Approach**

As a former **West Chester East lacrosse coach** and a school counselor, I've seen firsthand that traditional "grind harder" coaching often backfires for neurodivergent athletes. My approach to sports performance coaching isn't about running more laps; it's about **psychological strategy**.

#### **1. The 24-Hour Rule**

One of the core strategies we use in my practice is the **24-Hour Rule**. For athletes who struggle with emotional regulation, we establish a strict boundary: You have 24 hours to feel the "sting" of a loss or the "high" of a win. After that, we use specific "Expertise Cards" to pivot that energy into actionable data for the next game. This prevents the "shame spiral" that often derails an ADHD athlete's week.

#### **2. Using "Expertise Cards" for Mental Sharpness**

I provide my athletes with tactical, ADHD-informed tools I call **Expertise Cards**. These are bite-sized, actionable psychological strategies designed to be used in the heat of competition. Whether it's a grounding technique for a pitcher who just walked the bases loaded or a focus-reset for a soccer player after a bad call, these tools turn "trying harder" into "playing smarter."

### **Supporting the "Blue Demon" Journey**

Whether your child is a rising star at **Kennett Middle School** or a senior looking to play at the collegiate level, the transition between seasons and academic years can be a minefield.

I work with families across **Kennett Square, Chadds Ford, and Unionville** to ensure that the skills learned on the field—resilience, teamwork, and strategy—actually translate to the classroom. We don't just want them to be better athletes; we want them to be more confident, self-advocating young adults.
